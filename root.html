This is the digital portfolio of Jade Rager, a full-stack student enrolled in the Codefellows training program.

We're not living in the 210th decade any more. The web has quickly become a symbol of universal access to information.

So shouldn't it be universally accessible, built with a wide array of mobile devices in mind?

As a coder with deep interest in and passion for the internet, both as an indispensible tool for the modern human, and as a technology with vast and unexplored potential for the future, I think it should be.

Responsive Web Design

Smartphones. Tablets. Even desktop monitors may have a swivel mount.

The web must be viewport-agnostic.

It's a wonder that it ever wasn't - resizable windows are foundational to all desktop operating systems.

Mobile is not a trend that can or should be stopped, and certain developments are blurring the markets between desktop and mobile even further.

About Jade:
A Personal Bio

I owe the better half of my most formative moments to the internet. phpBB messageboards, flash animations, and a virtual reality platform by the name of ActiveWorlds make up a sizable portion of the nostalgia pie.

The allure of being able to create the very same things that I voraciously consumed was too much to resist as a child, and I made many forays into everything from HTML/CSS, to Python, Ruby, and even general-purpose application languages like Java and C++. Equipped with only online tutorials, my resources were limited then, and while I made quite a few demos, I never acquired the needed know-how to realize a major project. There is only so much an aspiring coder can do with a static, unscripted HTML page and a collection of command-line C++ applications. Servers? Backends? Databases? A bit out-of-reach for fourteen-year-old-me.

Deterred but not defeated, I held tightly to the vision of myself as a technician and a creator. In truth, I couldn't let it go. That desire had taken root far too deeply to be dismissed, and it would not rest. I was already too enamored by the idea of being able to make things which solved problems no one else had before me, things that could be clever or even beautiful.

So, it was difficult to be pushed by life in other directions, to have that longing unmet, when at my very core I had constructed my identity entirely around the premise that I was a coder, and placed all my hope into that future. The limitations on what I had available to me coupled with my apprehending the sheer scope of what I had to learn - unassisted - as a first-generation tech-worker in a rural backwater town did eventually set in, however...

My first heartbreak was not with any person but with a calling. As happens with these stories, I awaited the day I could return to what I loved. (Aww!)

Uncharacteristically for one of these stories, I did in fact re-unite with what I sought in the first place. My skillset is vastly broader than anything I knew before, and my younger self would likely be in awe or disbelief of what I've accomplished today. It's a nice chapter to stick your bookmark in, don't you think?
